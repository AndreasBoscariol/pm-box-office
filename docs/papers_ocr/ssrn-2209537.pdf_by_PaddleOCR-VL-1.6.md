Is Search Volume a Good Predictor?

Product Quality and the Predictive Performance of Search Volume

## ABSTRACT

This study explains the varying predictive power of online search volume by examining the impact of perceived quality and quality uncertainty of products on search volume and demand. We develop hypotheses regarding the relationship among the quality variables, online search activities, and actual demand and test them in the empirical setting of the U.S. movie industry. We propose different observable variables to operationalize consumers' perceived quality and quality uncertainty over a movie's life-cycle. We relate the observable quality variables with revenue and online search volume in two systems of simultaneous equations: one for the opening week and the other for the subsequent weeks. We find that both quality and quality uncertainty are positively associated with the pre-launch search volume of movies whereas neither of them directly influences opening-week revenue. Similar findings are maintained in the analysis of post-launch weeks: weekly measurements of quality and quality uncertainty are positively associated with weekly search volume, but not with weekly revenue. These findings imply that systematic over-/under-prediction of market success may occur if we use an online search index without considering the effects of quality and quality uncertainty. This study also makes two methodological contributions. First, we propose a practical way of measuring quality-related constructs in the pre-launch period of movies when even professional reviews are not available. Second, we devise a novel method to convert Google search indices to cross-sectionally comparable search volume measures.

Keywords: online search volume, Google search index, demand prediction, quality, quality uncertainty, movie industry.

## INTRODUCTION

Previous studies on the predictive performance of collective consumer behavior on the Internet seem to provide two conflicting conclusions. One group of studies finds that the collective online behavior of consumers, especially online search activity, provides excellent predictive performance for new-product demand. The industry sectors examined include the housing industry (Wu and Brynjolfsson 2009), financial markets (Mao, Counts, and Bollen 2011), the movie industry (Goel et al., 2010; Kulkarni, Kannan, and Moe 2012), the video game industry, and the music industry (Goel et al., 2010). On the other hand, the second group of studies report that the predictive performance of collective activity of online users varies across products (Mestyan, Yasseri, and Kertesz 2012; Wong, Sen, and Chiang 2012).

This study aims to explain when collective consumer action on the Internet is a good predictor for new-product demand versus when it is not. The research question we address is when the online search volume reflects the collective purchase intent of consumers well, and when it does not. To answer the research question, we focus on the role of quality constructs such as the perceived quality and the perceived uncertainty about the quality of products in generating consumer search activities and actual demand. The intuition is that products of high uncertain quality may experience high search from consumers (Moorthy, Ratchford, and Talukdar 1997) but the high search intensity motivated by uncertain quality is not necessarily translated into sales. This will be especially so if the search activity reveals that the quality of the product is indeed low.

We hypothesize that consumers' perceived quality uncertainty of a product increases the search volume, but not necessarily the demand, for the product. We also hypothesize that the

perceived quality of a product influences the relationship between online search volume and market demand. That is, the higher the perceived quality of a product is, the more effectively search volume of the product is translated into demand. We test our theory in the U.S. movie industry. Quality is an abstract concept, and there exist different states of quality of a product over its life-cycle (Golder, Mitra, and Moorman 2012). As such, we utilize different observable variables to operationalize consumers' perceived quality and quality uncertainty about a movie over its life-cycle. Then we relate the observable quality variables with revenue and online search volume in two systems of simultaneous equations, one for the opening week and the other for the subsequent weeks. We first find supporting evidence for our hypotheses on opening-week revenue and pre-launch search volume: 1) both perceived quality and quality uncertainty increase pre-launch search volume of movies whereas neither of them is directly associated with opening-week revenue; 2) the perceived quality—plausibly as a result of search—influences the effectiveness of search volume in generating demand. Then we extend our analysis to the post-launch period and find the same results by use of different operationalizations of quality and quality uncertainty. Our findings imply that managers should interpret with caution the market trend read from online search volume (e.g., Du and Kamakura 2012) and the market demand predicted by use of online search volume (e.g., Kulkarni, Kannan, and Moe 2012).

In answering the focal research question, we also find other noteworthy facts about the

effects of advertising and the determinants of critic ratings of movies. Regarding advertising

effects, we find that the same level of advertising generates more revenue for movies whose

quality is perceived to be more uncertain, due to the positive effect of quality uncertainty on

search volume and the positive effect of search volume on demand. As for the determinants of

critic ratings of a movie, the past performance of the movie's director is the most influential determinant of its average critic rating.

Our contribution is both substantive and methodological. Substantively, we provide an explanation for why online search volume, which is receiving tremendous attention for its ability to provide business intelligence, is not a good predictor of market demand for certain products even within the same industry. New-product managers may want to use our findings to better forecast the market demand of their products. Methodological contributions are two fold. First, we propose a practical way of measuring quality-related constructs in the movie industry as a movie goes through different phases of its life-cycle from the pre-launch to the post-launch period. Managers can use the readily available data from IMDb.com and Yahoo Movies to measure quality-related constructs of movies. Second, we devise a novel method of constructing cross-sectionally comparable search volume measures from the readily available Google search indices, which are not cross-sectionally comparable due to Google’s data normalization policy (Google Trends 2012).

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. We review relevant research and develop our

theory. Then we introduce our movie data and overview key features of the data. We develop a

system of dynamic simulation equations and estimate the model. Several managerial implications

are provided. We then formulate conclusions and areas for future research.

##### RELEVANT LITERATURE AND THEORY DEVELOPMENT

### Quality Perception, Search Volume, and Demand

Perceived Quality and Search Volume. Previous research implies that the online search volume of a product increases with consumers' perceived quality of the product (Beatty and Smith 1987;

Punj and Staelin 1983; Srinivasan and Ratchford 1991). This is because, all else being equal,
higher quality products generate more interest among consumers, which leads to more
information-seeking activities for that product. Therefore, if consumers can infer or perceive the
quality of products from some observable sources, those sources can influence the search volume
that the products receive. There will be a positive association between the perceived quality and
the search volume of products, all else being equal.

Previous research also implies that perceived uncertainty about a product’s quality can influence the search volume that the product receives. Moorthy, Ratchford, and Talukdar (1997) find that perceived uncertainty of brand increases the amount of search. Hess (1982)’s normative model of cost and benefit of search proposes that uncertainty about the payoffs from alternatives increases the expected returns of search, which in turn increases the amount of search effort. Chatterjee (2001) finds that consumers who are interested in a less popular product (such as niche products) are likely to search more. Because less popular products are collectively less familiar and thus more uncertain to the consumer population as a whole, her finding proposes that collectively more searches will be observed for products of higher uncertainty. In the same vein, Zhu and Zhang (2010) find that online reviews are more effective in influencing the purchases of less popular products because consumers are more likely to seek quality information to minimize the purchase risk. Therefore, if consumers can a priori infer the quality uncertainty of a product from available sources, those sources can influence the amount of search that the product receives from consumers. Products whose inferred quality uncertainty is higher will receive more searches from consumers, all else being equal.

Conversion of Search into Demand. Provided that search activities reflect interest in the searched products, a certain portion of search activities will be converted into actual demand.

The conversion of search into demand may be affected by the quality information consumers acquire as a result of their search activities. For example, consumers search for various information sources such as professional reviews, other consumers' reviews, and product Web pages before making purchase decisions, and they will purchase the searched product only when they conclude that the quality of the product is sufficiently high. Therefore, while consumers' search activities are initiated by their a priori perception on quality and quality uncertainty of the focal product, the translation from search activity into sales may be moderated by the information acquired by search.

In sum, consumer search activity works as a mediator between quality perception and actual purchase, where a priori perceived quality and uncertainty about quality of a product influences only search volume, but not actual demand. The updated quality perception after search moderates the relationship between the search volume and demand. Figure 1 illustrates our theory.

### Quality Perception on Movies

Product quality and price are critical determinants of market demand for products. In the movie industry, where price differentiation is not a common marketing tool, the quality of a movie seems to be the most influential factor for the movie’s market success. As Golder, Mitra, and Moorman (2012) propose, quality is a complex construct, and there may exist different states or operationalizations of quality. In the production stage of a product, the quality of a product is described by the produced attributes; in the experience stage where firms deliver the product to consumers, the attributes of the product are subjectively perceived through consumers’ own lens.

finally in the evaluation stage, consumers generate a summary statement of their experience with the product in the form of reviews and ratings.

In terms of the sources of consumer quality perception, Golder, Mitra, and Moorman (2012) propose that consumers infer quality of a product from a variety of sources ranging from product attributes to professional reviews and peer consumers' opinions, depending on the availability of those sources. If we apply this notion to the quality perception of movies, we can acquire insights on the sources that consumers may rely on to form their perception on the quality of movies. First, in the pre-launch period of movies where neither experienced quality nor evaluated quality exists, consumers may form an a priori perception on the quality of a movie based on the movie's product characteristics. For example, consumers may consider the participation of high-profile stars and directors (Hennig-Thurau, Houston, and Sridhar 2006) and their past performance to infer the quality and the uncertainty about the quality of a movie in its pre-launch period.

While consumers may reasonably conjecture quality of movies from movie attributes, the experiential nature of movie consumption makes it difficult to evaluate movie quality until it is consumed. When the quality of a product is difficult to evaluate before consumption, consumers seek other people's opinions to obtain quality information (Bolton, Katok, and Ockenfels 2004; Clemons, Gao, and Hitt 2006; Pavlou and Gefen 2004). As such, when reviews become available, consumers will consider the review information to update their a priori assessment about movie quality that was made solely from movie characteristics. Likewise, when user reviews become available, consumers will take them into account to form their perception on movie quality. Because consumers and professional critics emphasize different criteria when determining movie

quality (Holbrook 1999), consumers may weigh more on other consumers' experiences than professional critics' opinions when both are available.

### Observable Quality Variables

As quality and uncertainty about quality are abstract constructs, we operationalize them with

observable variables for our empirical analysis. As consumers may rely on different information

sources depending on the availability of the sources, we use different variables to operationalize

product quality over the movie life-cycle.

Pre-Launch Period and the Opening Week. In the pre-launch period of movies, consumers have neither user opinions nor professional reviews. In this period, consumers have no other option but to use movie characteristics to infer quality. In particular, high-profile stars and directors of a movie can signal to consumers that the movie is of high quality (Basuroy, Desai, and Talukdar 2006). For example, consumers may expect that a movie directed by Steven Spielberg is of higher quality than a movie directed by a less successful director. Likewise, consumers may infer that the quality of a movie will be high if well-known stars such as Tom Cruise or Brad Pitt appear in the movie. To extend this notion, when no review information is available, consumers may consider the past performance and filmography of the directors and stars of a movie to infer the quality and quality uncertainty of the movie. Therefore, we use the past performance of the directors and stars of a focal movie to operationalize its pre-launch quality and quality uncertainty. Specifically to operationalize the pre-launch quality of a movie, we use the mean user ratings of past movies with which the directors and stars of the focal movie have been involved. To operationalize the pre-launch quality uncertainty about a movie, we use the standard deviation of ratings of past movies with which the directors and stars of the focal movie have been involved.

Post-Launch Period. The experiential nature of movie consumption makes consumers rely heavily on review information to aid their movie-consumption decisions. To a potential movie consumer, user reviews are considered an important source for making the movie-going decision (Chintagunta, Gopinath, and Venkataraman 2010; Holbrook 1999). Therefore, in the post-launch period, we use user reviews to measure the quality of movies. Specifically, we use average weekly user ratings (i.e., WOM valence) of the previous week to operationalize the perceived quality of a movie in a certain week. We use standard deviation of weekly ratings of a movie to operationalize the perceived uncertainty (or disagreement among consumers) about the quality of the movie.

### Hypotheses

To summarize our theory, we propose the following hypotheses that relate the measurable

variables of quality constructs with search volume and revenue. Hypotheses H1 through H5 deal

with the pre-launch and opening-week relationships between movie quality, search volume, and

revenue. H6 through H9 hypothesize the post-launch relationships between movie quality, search,

and weekly revenue.

H1: The pre-launch search volume of a new movie is positively associated with the average user rating of the past movies that were made by the focal movie's director; it is also positively associated with the average user rating of the past movies in which the focal movie's actors/actresses starred.

H2: The pre-launch search volume of a new movie is positively associated with the standard deviation of user ratings of the past movies that were made by the focal movie's director; it is also positively associated with the standard deviation of user ratings of the past movies in which the focal movie's actors/actresses starred.

H3: The opening-week revenue of a movie is NOT directly associated with the average user rating of the past movies that were made by the focal movie's director; also, it is NOT directly associated with the average user rating of the past movies in which the focal movie's actors/actresses starred.

H4: The opening-week revenue of a movie is NOT directly associated with the standard deviation of user ratings of the past movies that were made by the focal movie's director; also, it is NOT directly associated with the standard deviation of user ratings of the past movies in which the focal movie's actors/actresses starred.

H5: The pre-launch search volume is more effectively translated into the opening-week revenue for a movie with higher critic ratings.

H0: In the post-launch period, the previous week's average user rating of a movie is positively associated with the current week's search volume of the movie.

H7: In the post-launch period, the previous week's standard deviation of user ratings is positively associated with the current week's search volume.

H8: In the post-launch period, the previous week's standard deviation of user ratings is NOT directly associated with the current week's revenue.

H9: In the post-launch period, a movie's search volume in a week is more effectively translated into that week's revenue when the previous week's user rating is higher.

#### Constructing Cross-Sectionally Comparable Search Volume Measure

Google Trends provides weekly search index of keyword queries entered to Google search engine. Because the index is normalized to conceal the actual search volume of the keyword, researchers cannot compare the search volumes across different keywords if the raw search index is used as provided by Google Trends. We avoid this problem with a method that transforms the raw search indexes from Google into cross-sectionally comparable search volume measures. The essence of the method is to select a set of basis keywords and transform the search indexes of movie keywords based on that of the basis keywords. The method consists of three steps. The first step is the keyword selection step where basis keywords and movie keywords are selected. Any set of words can be selected for the basis keywords. The only requirement is that the search

volume is neither to be too high nor too low compared with the search volume of the focal

movies. For our analysis, we select the following seven basis keywords: “mac os”, lamp, hello,

windows, weather, tomatoes, video, imdb. They are listed in the order of search amount in the

U.S. movie industry, according to Google Trends. That is, among the eight keywords, “mac os”

is the least searched keyword and “imdb” is the most searched keyword in the U.S. movie

industry. Then for each movie, we select a set of keywords that are considered to be used by

consumers to search the movie. For example, for movie 12 Rounds, we choose “12 Rounds” as

the keyword for the movie. For movie Paul Blart: Mall Cop, we choose “blart + mall cop”,

which means either blart or “mall cop” $ ^{2} $. Figure 2 shows the pairwise comparisons of the weekly

search volume indexes of a basis keyword with its adjacent one from August 2007 to January

2010.

The second step is the keyword matching step. To each movie, we assign an appropriate basis keyword, and collect the Google search index of the movie keyword along with that of the assigned basis keyword to the movie. Any basis keyword can be assigned to any movie as long as the search index of the movie keyword is comparable to that of the chosen basis keyword for the movie. That is, if the search volume of a certain basis keyword is too large compared to the search volume of a movie keyword, that basis keyword should not be used for that movie because the movie's search index so collected will be shrunk to be zero for many or all weeks. Google Trends provides diverse filters to minimize the measurement error in collecting intended

search index. We limit our search so that the search volume is measured only from the U.S. movie industry.

The last step is the transformation step. We transform each movie’s search index into our cross-sectionally comparable search volume measure. The mathematics behind this step can be explained as follows. Let  $ k_j $ be the basis keyword at the j’th position (i.e.,  $ k_1 = “mac os” $,  $ k_2 = lamp $, ...,  $ k_8 = imdb $), and let  $ \mathbf{I}_t^{k_j} $ represent the search index of the j’th basis keyword at week t. #### EMPIRICAL ANALYSES

To test our hypotheses, we develop a system of dynamic simultaneous equations that closely follows the work of Elberse and Eliashberg (2003). We use a multiplicative formulation and distinguish between a movie’s opening week and its run in subsequent weeks. In the model, index j represents movie j and index t represents week t. The release week is denoted by t = 0; k weeks before release is represented by t = -k; and k weeks after release is represented by t = k.

##### The Post-Launch Model

The post-launch model is similarly constructed by replacing the variables in the opening-week model by their corresponding counterparts available in the post-launch periods. Specifically, we use the previous week's average user rating and standard deviation of user ratings to operationalize the weekly varying quality and quality uncertainty of a movie. Using the weekly varying variables for quality constructs enables us to test whether our theory holds even within the same movie as the quality-related information changes over time. ##### Estimation

We use a three-stage least-squares (3SLS) method to estimate the system of equations (2) through (10). A ordinary least squares (OLS) method is inconsistent because some covariates are endogenous. Also, a two-stage least-squares (2SLS) method is less efficient than a 3SLS method as the errors across equations may be correlated (Zeller and Theil 1962). For the opening-week model, equations (2) through (5) are estimated as a system and the variables CSearch $ _{j,t-1} $, Revenue $ _{j,0} $, Scrns $ _{j,0} $, and AvgCriticRating $ _{j} $ are treated as endogenous variables. For the post-launch model, equations (6) through (10) are estimated as a system where the variables Search $ _{j,t} $, Revenue $ _{j,t} $, Scrns $ _{j,t} $, AvgUserRating $ _{j,t} $, and SdUserRating $ _{j,t} $ are treated as endogenous variables. When estimating the post-launch model, we exclude the first-lag endogenous variables from the set of instrumental variables to reduce potential estimation problems related to serial correlation (Elberse and Eliashberg 2003). Instead, as in Elberse and Eliashberg (2003), we employ the set of time-invariant exogenous variables that are used to estimate the opening-week model. We also use the second-order lagged endogenous variables of the weekly average rating and the standard

deviation of weekly average ratings, as they cause less serious problems for identification. The time-specific fixed effects are accounted for by week dummy variables in the post-launch model.

Let us discuss the effect of our focal variables with the 3SLS results. First, regarding the determinants of search volume in the pre-launch period, not only the average user rating of the focal director’s past movies but also the standard deviation of user ratings of the focal director’s past movies are positively associated with consumers’ online search actions. As movie attributes are the only available information for consumers to form their assessment on movie quality at this stage, this finding implies that consumers’ perception on a movie’s quality uncertainty as well as its quality influences their search actions in the pre-launch period. Note, however, that neither quality nor quality uncertainty of a movie directly increases opening-week revenue; rather they are associated with opening-week revenue only indirectly through search volume. Second, the positive interaction effect of search volume and average critic rating on opening-week revenue indicates that the information-seeking behavior of consumers, i.e., online search, is better translated into demand when consumers are ensured about the quality of the movie through professional reviews.

The above findings provide empirical evidences for our hypotheses H1 through H5, which explain why online search volume alone is not a good predictor of market demand for certain movies. Especially, if a movie's director has experienced high disagreement among consumers in the past (as measured by the standard deviation of user ratings of the past movies) and if the

movie suffers from low professional ratings, online search volume of the movie can over-predict the movie's opening-week revenue.

Our study finds the determinants of critic ratings of movies (Table 4(d)). The average critic rating of a movie is positively associated with the past performance of the director of the movie, specifically the total U.S. box-office revenue and the average user rating of the past movies of the focal movie’s director. On the other hand, the average critic rating is negatively associated with the pre-launch advertising, major distributor, and sequel movies. As the determinants of critic ratings are observable long before a movie’s release time, this finding provides managers an early prediction for a movie’s critic ratings. More importantly, as critic ratings moderate the conversation rate from pre-launch search to opening-week revenue, this finding implies that the conversation rate from pre-launch search volume to opening-week revenue can be predicted early in a movie’s pre-launch period. As far as we know, this is the first study that finds the determinants of critic ratings of movies.

Other noteworthy findings are as follows. Pre-launch advertising increases pre-launch

consumer search activity, and sequel movies tend to receive more attentions (i.e., searches) from

consumers than original movies. A studio’s decision to make a sequel movie signals that the

sequel movie is of high quality in the eye of ordinary consumers (Basuroy, Desai, and Talukdar

2006), making more consumers interested in the sequel movie. The number of screens, pre-

launch interests (as measured by pre-launch search volume), and opening-week advertising are

pivotal determinants of opening-week revenue. In particular, pre-launch search is found to be

more effective than opening-week advertising in generating revenue. $ ^{6} $

##### MANAGERIAL IMPLICATIONS

The findings provide important implications for managers. First, systematic over-/under-

prediction of box-office revenue is likely if we rely on online search volume without accounting

for the quality uncertainty. Over-prediction is highly likely if a new movie’s director has

experienced high standard deviation of user ratings for his or her past movies, and at the same

time, the new movie’s average critic rating is low. As an early sign for the professional reviews a

new movie will receive, managers can use the determinants of critic ratings that are found in this

study—namely, the past performance of the focal director, major distributor dummy, and

whether the new movie is a sequel. In the same vein, systematic under-prediction of box-office

revenue can happen for movies with the opposite characteristic—i.e., the new movie’s director

has shown stable performance in terms of the standard deviation of user ratings, and at the same

time, the movie’s average critic rating is high. Good news is that this systematic over-/under-

prediction is predictable and can be mitigated.

Second, the same level of advertising ends up generating more revenue for movies with a higher level of user disagreement about quality (i.e., the standard deviation of user ratings of past movies of the focal directors in the opening week and the standard deviation of user ratings in the subsequent weeks), all else being equal. This is because, for movies of higher quality uncertainty, the same level of advertising creates more search activities (equations (2) and (3)), which is, in turn, associated with more revenue (equations (4) and (5)). In light of consumer behavior theory, this is related to the notion of awareness effect and mere exposure effect that are created by consumers' search activities (Duan, Gu, and Whinston 2008; Janiszewski 1993; Liu 2006; Zajonc 1968).

Third, the conversion rate from pre-launch search volume into opening-week revenue can be predicted in the early pre-launch period as the determinants found in our study are observable long before a new movie's release.

Fourth, as a practical way of measuring quality uncertainty, we propose to use the standard deviation of user ratings of past movies of the focal director and the standard deviation of user ratings of the previous week for the opening week and the subsequent weeks, respectively.

Lastly, keyword selection is crucial to accurately measuring consumers' online search activities. Too strict a rule for keyword selection can underestimate actual consumer interest while too loose a rule can overestimate marketing effects. Indeed, finding appropriate keywords is a realm of art and it may need industry experts who are knowledgeable about actual keyword selection by consumers. For this reason, if the objective of the study is to read market trends, it is recommended to collect multiple time-series and extract underlying latent factors that co-move the multiple time-series (Du and Kamakura 2012).

## CONCLUSIONS

While collective consumer activities on the Internet have been considered a good predictor of demand, it is also reported to have limited predictive ability for certain products. This study shows that such limited predictive performance also happens with the Google search index—readily available data that is found to be an excellent means to acquire business intelligence—and provides an explanation on why aggregate online search volume is not a good predictor for certain products. At the core of our theory lies quality-related constructs such as perceived quality and quality uncertainty. Specifically, we hypothesize that the perceived quality uncertainty directly influences the amount of search but not the actual demand. Also, we emphasize the moderating role of perceived quality in the conversion of search action into demand. We find empirical support for our theory in the U.S. movie industry. Other noteworthy findings include the determinants of a movie’s average critic rating.

We also make methodological contributions. We propose a practical way of measuring quality-related constructs in the movie industry, especially in the early pre-launch period where no reviews are available. Also, we devise a novel method of constructing cross-sectionally comparable search volume measures from readily available Google search indices.

Our study provides valuable managerial implications. First, it diagnoses which movies are more prone to over-/under-prediction when managers use online search volume for prediction purposes. As such, the systematic over-prediction problem of movie revenue can be predicted and mitigated. Second, our study suggests that movie studios take a look at the quality uncertainty variables to allocate their advertising budget across different movies. Third, the conversion rate from search into sales can be predicted early by use of available public

information. Lastly, the study suggests that finding appropriate construction rules for keywords is critical.

This study also has limitations. First of all, our findings are specific to the movie industry.

Extending to other industries will be necessary to generalize our theory. Second, as with any

study using online data is, our study is subject to measurement error, especially the online search

volume measure. While we show that our search volume measure is reasonable by showing its

superior correlation with revenue, the search volume is subject to the keywords that we choose.

In fact, any study that uses search volume—whether the data is at the aggregate level or the

individual consumer level—has this measurement problem. In this sense, finding an appropriate

rule of keyword selection can be an intriguing research topic.

There are many interesting future research topics that use readily available search volume data. First, we can consider combining online search volume with other data sources to better measure consumers' purchase intent. For example, to better forecast the demand of a new product, we can combine search volume with primary data sources that directly measure purchase intention, consumption interest, and WOM valence. Second, online search volume can be used to measure the effectiveness of marketing activities that have been impossible to measure in the past due to a lack of the appropriate response variables. For example, weekly search volume in the pre-launch period of new products can be used to measure the effectiveness of pre-launch advertising. Another example is to estimate latent demands of certain products across different geographic regions (e.g., DMAs) if the online search volume can be measured at the geographic market level. Third, studying the joint effect of online search and online opinion posting can be an appealing topic. Online search is related to reading information while online opinion posting is related to writing information. While there is ample research on the effect of

online reviews on market outcomes, there is little research so far that jointly examines the effect of online reading and writing.
